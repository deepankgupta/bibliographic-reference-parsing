<p>ORIGINAL ARTICLE</p>
<p>A new species, Dicheirinia panamensis, and new<br>records of rust fungi from Panama</p>
<p>Jose R. Hernandez • Meike Piepenbring •<br>Maritza Betzaida Vega Rios</p>
<p>Received: 13 July 2006/Revised: 15 November 2006/Accepted: 19 January 2007/Published online: 11 April 2007<br>&copy; German Mycological Society and Springer-Verlag 2007</p>
<p>Abstract Based on a recent fieldwork in Panama, 25 species<br>of rust fungi and several new hosts are reported for the first<br>time from this country. Among the new records is one new<br>species, Dicheirinia panamensis on Cojoba rufescens<br>(Fabaceae). It differs from known species in the genus<br>Dicheirinia by the presence of uredinia and telia without<br>paraphyses, irregularly tuberculate urediniospores with two<br>germ pores on the flattened sides, and tuberculate telio-<br>spores formed by three probasidial cells, subtended by a<br>pedicel with three hyaline, apical cells.</p>
<p>Introduction</p>
<p>Among the first fungi reported from Panama were rusts<br>collected by N.G. Lagerheim (Dietel 1894). Since then,<br>data on rust fungi (Uredinales, Basidiomycota) from Panama<br>have been published by numerous authors: Arthur (1918),</p>
<p>Jackson (1926), Standley (1927), Kern and Chardon<br>(1927), Kern (1938), Hennen and Cummins (1956), J0rstad<br>(1957), Toler et al. (1959), Ramachar and Cummins (1965),<br>Cummins (1978), Buritica and Hennen (1980), Ono and<br>Hennen (1983), Hennen and McCain (1993), Buritica</p>
<p>(1999a, b), Berndt (2002), Hernandez and Hennen (2003),</p>
<p>Hernandez et al. (n.d.), and Piepenbring (2005). In total,<br>only about 67 species of rust fungi are known from Panama<br>(Piepenbring 2006), although diversity of plants is very<br>high and rusts are common in this country.</p>
<p>Several days of intensive field work in Panama yielded<br>numerous new records of rust species, new host records,<br>and a species of Dicheirinia on Fabaceae that is different<br>from all known species and therefore described as new.</p>
<p>Materials and methods</p>
<p>Fresh specimens were collected from 24-29 November<br>2004 mainly in the province of Chiriqui, not only in agri-<br>cultural fields and natural topical forest but also on road-<br>sides between Panama City and Chiriqui. Collections were<br>pressed in a conventional plant press and slowly dried in an<br>electric drier. For microscopic examination, material was<br>mounted in aqueous lactic acid and examined using a Zeiss<br>Axioplan 2 microscope with bright-field optics. Size ranges<br>in species descriptions are based on at least 20 measure-<br>ments of each structure. Digital images were taken using a<br>Nikon Coolpix 995 and a Nikon DXM 1200. Drawings<br>were done with a drawing mirror or free hand on scaled<br>paper, redrawn with ink, and scanned. SEM pictures were<br>taken from spores dusted onto double-adhesive tape,<br>covered during 45 s at 5 kV with gold, and observed with<br>a Hitachi S4500. Plates were made using Adobe Photoshop<br>Elements 2.0 and Corel Draw 12. Specimens from BPI were</p>

Species of Dicheirinia differ<br>from species of the other genera by vertically septate,<br>tuberculate teliospores, subtended by a hyaline pedicel with<br>one apical cell for each probasidial cell. The type species of<br>the genus is D. binata (Berk. and M.A. Curtis) Arthur (=<br>Triphragmium binatum Berk. and M.A. Curtis) on Eryth-<br>rina sp. from Nicaragua. Twelve species of Dicheirinia<br>have been described on the three subfamilies of Fabaceae<br>(Cummins and Hiratsuka 2003): Caesalpinioideae (Cassia),<br>Faboideae (Abrus, Chamaecytisus, Cytisus, Erythrina, Lon-<br>chocarpus, Ormosia, Piptadenia,and Teline), and Mimosoi-</p>
<p>deae (Albizia, Archidendropsis,andLeucaena). Up to now,<br>no other rusts have been reported on the host species C.<br>rufescens. Dicheirinia panamensis differs from other species<br>in the genus by the presence of flattened, tuberculate<br>urediniospores of irregular shape with two germ pores and<br>teliospores with three probasidial cells.</p>

<p>New records</p>
<p>Twenty-five new records of rust fungi for Panama are listed<br>below, which bring the total of rusts known from Panama to<br>92. The considerable number of new records obtained,<br>based on specimens collected in less than 1 week, suggests<br>that we are still far from knowing the complete diversity of<br>rusts in Panama. In Costa Rica, a neighboring country with<br>similar plant diversity where rusts have been collected<br>much more intensively than in Panama, 292 species are</p>
<p>known (Berndt 2004).<br>New records of rusts in Panama</p>
