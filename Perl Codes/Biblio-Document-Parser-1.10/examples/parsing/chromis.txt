<p>Abstract</p>
<p>Five new species of the damselfish genus Chromis2 (Perciformes3: Labroidei4: Pomacentridae5) are described from speci-<br>mens collected from deep (&gt;60 m) coral-reef habitat in the western Pacific by divers using mixed-gas closed-circuit<br>rebreather gear. Two of the five new species (C. abyssus and C. circumaurea) are each described from specimens taken at<br>a single locality within the Caroline Islands (Palau and Yap, respectively); one (C. degruyi) is described from specimens<br>collected or observed throughout the Caroline Islands, and two (C. brevirostris and C. earina) are described from speci-<br>mens collected from several localities throughout the Caroline Islands, Fiji, and Vanuatu. All five species can easily be<br>distinguished from other known Chromis, and from each other, on the basis of color and morphology. These new species<br>represent the first five scientific names prospectively registered in the official ICZN ZooBank registry6. Moreover, the<br>electronic online edition of this document has been specially formatted with many embedded links to additional<br>resources available online via the internet to enhance access to taxonomically-relevant information, and as a demonstra-<br>tion of the utility of international standards for biodiversity informatics.</p>
<p>Key words: Chromis abyssus, Chromis brevirostris, Chromis circumaurea, Chromis degruyi, Chromis earina, new spe-<br>cies, taxonomy, cybertaxonomy, mesophotic, mixed-gas diving, rebreather, ZooBank</p>
<p>Introduction</p>
<p>Over the past two decades, the authors have developed and refined techniques for using advanced dive gear<br>and various gas mixtures to allow safe excursions to depths of 50–150 m, which exceeds the depths that can<br>be safely reached using conventional SCUBA. Initial exploratory deep dives by the authors at various locali-<br>ties throughout the tropical Pacific have revealed a rich diversity of coral-reef-associated fishes inhabiting</p>

<p>these depths (Pyle 1996a, 1996b, 1999, 2000). In addition to the discovery of many new species of fishes, ini-<br>tial exploration of deep coral reefs (sometimes referred to as “mesophotic” reefs) has led to new insights con-<br>cerning ecological and biogeographic processes in both the Pacific (Pyle 2000, unpublished data) and the<br>tropical Atlantic (Feitoza et al. 2005).</p>
<p>The pomacentrid genus Chromis Cuvier 1814 (type species Sparus7 chromis8 Linnaeus 1758) is the largest<br>genus of the family, with 86 valid species. Fishes of this genus are among the few pomacentrid species known<br>to inhabit deep coral reefs, perhaps a reflection of their principal food (zooplankton), in contrast to species of<br>genera that are dependant primarily on benthic algae. At least 34 species of Chromis are known to inhabit<br>depths of 50 m or more, but only nine are restricted to such depths, and only five (C. okamurai9 Yamakawa<br>and Randall 1989, C. mirationis10 Tanaka 1917, C. struhsakeri11 Randall and Swerdloff 1973, C. abyssicola12<br>Allen and Randall 1985, and C. onumai13 Senou and Kudo 2007) are known only from depths of 60 m or<br>more. Except for two specimens of C. brevirostris collected at a depth of 60 m, all specimens of the new spe-<br>cies described herein were collected at depths of 85 m or more.</p>
<p>Whereas the print (paper) edition of this article represents the publication through which these five new<br>scientific names are made available under the current (4th Edition) International Commission of Zoological<br>Nomenclature (ICZN)14 Code of Nomenclature15, the concurrently distributed electronic online edition is<br>intended to exemplify the utility of modern international biodiversity informatics standards. Symbolically<br>published on the 250th anniversary of the officially recognized date of publication of Systema Naturae (Lin-<br>naeus 1758), it is our hope that this article will help demonstrate the value of electronic informatics standards<br>in enhancing the work of all taxonomists throughout the next quarter-millennium and beyond.</p>
<p>Material and methods</p>
<p>Specimen collection and deposition</p>
<p>All specimens were collected with hand nets (sometimes with the aid of rotenone or quinaldine sulphate)<br>by divers using Cis-Lunar&reg; MK-5P mixed-gas, closed-circuit rebreathers, generally following protocols out-<br>lined by Pyle (1996c). In all cases, specimens were collected in full accordance with local government regula-<br>tions, and in compliance with appropriate animal care standards. Most specimens were photographed when<br>fresh, usually following the technique described by Randall (1961). Before exposure to formalin, tissue sam-<br>ples (usually the right pelvic fin) were removed from many specimens and stored in vials containing DMSO<br>for later analysis. Holotypes of C. abyssus, C. brevirostris, C. circumaurea, and C. degruyi are deposited in<br>the fish collection of the Bernice P. Bishop Museum, Honolulu (BPBM)16; the holotype of C. earina is depos-<br>ited in the fish collection of Muséum National d’Histoire Naturelle, Paris (MNHN)17. Paratypes are deposited<br>in these two insitutions, as well as The Natural History Museum, London (BMNH)18; California Academy of<br>Sciences, San Francisco (CAS)19; United States National Museum of Natural History, Washington, D.C.<br>(USNM)20; and the Western Australian Museum, Perth (WAM)21. Tissue samples were deposited for long-term<br>cryogenic storage at the Pacific Center for Molecular Biodiversity (PCMB)22, located at BPBM. All of these<br>collections have been registered with the Biodiversity Collections Index (BCI)23.</p>
<p>Counts, measurements and molecular analysis</p>
<p>Counts of median fin rays, vertebrae, and predorsal bones were taken from digitally-scanned radiograph<br>images (“x-rays”)24. Measurements of standard length (SL), lengths from tip of snout to origins of dorsal, pel-<br>vic and anal fins, dorsal- and anal-fin bases, and dorsal- and anal-fin spine lengths of specimens not bent or<br>otherwise distorted were calculated to the nearest tenth (0.1) of a mm from radiograph images25, and verified<br>against measurements taken directly from specimens. All other counts and measurements were taken directly<br>from specimens using a Fowler&reg; Ultra-Cal IV 300-mm digital caliper. Measurement values were recorded to</p>
<p>4 · Zootaxa 1671 &copy; 2008 Magnolia Press&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;PYLE ET AL.</p>

<p>the nearest hundredth (0.01) of a mm, but rounded to the nearest tenth (0.1) of a mm for analyses. Methods of<br>counts and measurements are mostly consistent with those described in Allen &amp; Randall (2004).</p>
<p>Unless otherwise indicated, specimen lengths are SL26, measured as the straight-line distance between the<br>anteriormost tip of the upper lip and the posterior edge of the hypural plate; head length27 is between anterior-<br>most tip of upper lip and posterior edge of opercle flap; body depth28 is maximum vertical distance between<br>belly and base of dorsal spines; body width29 is maximum width posterior to opercular opening; snout length30<br>is straight-line distance from anteriormost tip of upper lip to closest point on anterior fleshy edge of orbit;<br>orbit diameter31 is horizontal fleshy diameter; interorbital width32 is least bony width between eyes; upper jaw<br>length33 is straight-line distance from anteriormost tip of upper lip to ventroposteriormost tip of maxilla; cau-<br>dal peduncle depth34 is least depth; caudal peduncle length is horizontal distance between verticals at posterior<br>base of anal fin and posterior edge of hypural plate35; fin spine36 and ray37 lengths are straight-line distances<br>between extreme base and distal tip (including filamentous extension, if any); caudal fin length38 is horizontal<br>distance from posterior edge of hypural plate to a vertical at distal tip of longest ray (including filamentous<br>extension, if any); caudal concavity39 is horizontal distance between tips of the shortest and longest rays; pec-<br>toral-fin length40 is length of longest ray; pelvic fin length41 is length of longest ray; pectoral-fin ray count42<br>includes small splint-like, uppermost rudimentary ray; lateral-line scale count43 includes only those scales with<br>tubes; a separate count is provided for pored or pitted scales occurring midlaterally on caudal peduncle44;<br>scale-row counts above45 and below46 lateral line to origins of dorsal and anal fins (respectively) with values<br>that include “.5” refer to small truncate scales (if any) at bases of respective fins; gill-raker counts47 include all<br>rudiments and are provided as separate values for upper and lower limbs of first gill arch; last fin ray of dorsal<br>and anal fins sometimes branched at or near extreme base and counted as a single ray, but only when con-<br>nected at or near the extreme base48 (otherwise counted as two49).</p>
<p>Unless otherwise indicated, measurements provided in the text represent proportion of SL. Measurements<br>provided in the tables represent percent of SL. Counts, measurements and proportions appearing in parenthe-<br>ses represent ranges for paratypes, if different from the holotype. Counts represented as two values separated<br>by a pipe “|” represent values taken from left|right sides of specimens.</p>
<p>Total genomic DNA was extracted from available tissue samples using the Qiagen Dneasy&reg; tissue kit fol-<br>lowing the manufacturer’s protocol. In compliance with the Barcode of Life initiative, a fragment of the cyto-<br>chrome oxidase I (COI) gene from the mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) was amplified for holotypes (and some<br>paratypes) of all species. Primers used for amplification and sequencing were BOL-F1 (5' TCA ACY AAT<br>CAY AAA GAT ATY GGC AC 3') and BOL-R1 (5' ACT TCY GGG TGR CCR AAR AAT CA 3'), modified<br>from Wa r d et al. (2005). Each PCR had a total volume of 25 µl, containing between 10–20 ng of purified<br>DNA, 2.5 µl of 10x reaction buffer, 1.5 µl of 8 mM pre-mixed dNTPs, 2.5 mM of MgCl2, 0.25 µM of each<br>primer and two units of Taq DNA polymerase (Promega, Madison, WI). Cycling parameters for the mtDNA<br>were as follows: initial denaturation at 94&deg;C for 2 min; 35 cycles of 94&deg;C for 45 sec, 50&deg;C for 45 sec, 72&deg;C for<br>55 sec; and a final extension at 72&deg;C for 2.5 min. The resulting reaction was purified with exonuclease I and<br>shrimp alkaline phosphatase enzymes. Excess oligonucleotide primers were removed through simultaneous<br>incubation of PCR product with exonuclease I and shrimp alkaline phosphatase (USB Corp., Cleveland OH).</p>
<p>Because of a broader ongoing effort by Luiz Rocha and colleagues to analyze phylogenetic patterns of<br>species within the genus Chromis, no attempt is made to infer evolutionary affinities of these five new species,<br>other than qualitative discussions about similarities with other species, if relevant.</p>

<p>Paratypes. BMNH 2007.10.31.165 (50.2 mm SL) [PCMB 310366]. BPBM 4085567 (3; 37.6–98.0 mm SL)<br>[PCMB 310068, 310269]. CAS 22575570 (64.1 mm SL) [PCMB 310571]. MNHN 2007.192272 (63.7 mm SL)<br>[PCMB 310473]. USNM 39113674 (2; 44.4–90.2 mm SL) [PCMB 310175]. WAM P.32898-00176 (64.5 mm SL)<br>[PCMB 310677]. All from same locality as holotype: sand and rubble slope with scattered rock outcroppings,<br>107–116 m, quinaldine and hand net, R.L. Pyle and B.D. Greene, 25 April 2007.</p>
<p>Diagnosis. Dorsal rays XIV,12–13 (usually 13); anal rays II,12–14 (usually 13); pectoral rays 18–19 (usu-<br>ally 19); spiniform caudal rays 3; tubed lateral-line scales 14–16; gill rakers 6–7+17–18 (usually 7+18; total<br>24–25, usually 25); body depth 1.58–1.83 in SL; color when fresh dark gray with a large iridescent dark blue<br>spot at center of each scale; membranes on median fins and pelvic fins opaque charcoal gray, with an irides-<br>cent dark blue margin on the spinous portion of the dorsal and anal fins; caudal fin mottled iridescent dark<br>blue and black; pectoral fins with a black ovoid spot covering the basal portion and pectoral-fin axil.</p>
<p>Description. Dorsal rays XIV,13 (two paratypes with XIV,12); anal rays II,13 (II,12–14); all dorsal and<br>anal rays branched, the last to base in some specimens; pectoral rays 19 (one paratype with 18), the upper 2<br>and lowermost unbranched; pelvic rays I,5; principal caudal rays 8+7=15; upper and lower procurrent caudal<br>rays 5, the anterior 3 spiniform, the posterior 2 segmented and unbranched; tubed lateral-line scales 16|14<br>(14–16 except for one paratype with 11|15); posterior midlateral scales with a pore or deep pit 7|6 (5–8);<br>scales above dorsal fin to origin of dorsal fin 3.5 (3–3.5); scales below lateral line to origin of anal fin 9<br>(9–10); gill rakers 7+18=25 (6–7+17–18=24–25), 6 gill rakers on upper limb of gill arch in one paratype, 17<br>gill rakers on lower limb of gill arch in one paratype); surpaneural (predorsal) bones 3; vertebrae 12+13.</p>
<p>Body moderately deep, depth 1.73 (1.58–1.83) in SL, and compressed, the width 3.23 (2.65–3.56) in body<br>depth; head length 3.01 (2.79–3.05) in SL; dorsal profile of head with slight convexity anterior to eye, slight<br>concavity dorsal to eye, and slight convexity on nape; snout shorter than orbit diameter, its length 4.11<br>(4.00–5.74) in head length; orbit diameter 2.66 (2.28–2.90) in head length; interorbital space convex, its width<br>2.79 (2.59–2.92) in head length; caudal-peduncle depth 2.17 (2.15–2.59) in head; caudal-peduncle length 3.01<br>(3.34–4.39) in head.</p>
<p>Mouth terminal, small, oblique, the upper jaw forming an angle of about 40º to horizontal axis of head and<br>body; posterior edge of maxilla reaching slightly beyond a vertical at anterior edge of pupil, the upper jaw<br>length 3.57 (3.39–4.11) in head; an outer row of conical teeth in each jaw, largest anteriorly; about 27 upper<br>and about 20 lower teeth on each side of jaw; a narrow band of villiform teeth lingual to outer row, in 2–3<br>irregular rows anteriorly, narrowing to a single row on side of jaws; tongue triangular with rounded tip; gill<br>rakers long and slender, the longest on lower limb near angle about four-fifths length of longest gill filaments;<br>nostril with a fleshy rim, more elevated on posterior edge and located at level of middle of pupil, slightly less<br>than one-third distance from front of snout to base of upper lip.</p>
<p>Opercle ending posteriorly in a flat spine, the tip relatively obtuse and obscured by a large scale; margin<br>of preopercle smooth, the posterior margin extending dorsally to level of upper edge of pupil; suborbital with<br>free lower margin extending nearly to a vertical at posterior edge of pupil.</p>
<p>Scales finely ctenoid; anterior lateral line ending beneath rear portion of spinous dorsal fin (between 11th<br>and 12th dorsal-fin spines); head scaled except lips, tip of snout, and a narrow zone from orbit to edge of snout<br>containing nostrils; a scaly sheath at base of dorsal and anal fins, about two-thirds pupil diameter at base of<br>middle of spinous portion of dorsal fin, progressively narrower on soft portion; a column of scales on each<br>membrane of dorsal fin, narrowing distally, those on spinous portion of dorsal progressively longer, reaching<br>about two-thirds distance to spine tips on posterior membranes; scales on anal-fin membrane in two columns,<br>progressively smaller distally; small scales on caudal fin extending slightly more than two-thirds distance to<br>posterior margin; small scales on basal one-fifth of pectoral fins; a median scaly process extending posteriorly<br>from between base of pelvic fins, its length about half that of pelvic spine; axillary scale above base of pelvic<br>spine about one-half length of spine. <p>Distribution. Only collected from the type locality; also observed at similar depths at Augulpelu Reef in<br>Palau. An individual of what appears to be this species was observed and photographed by Mr. Forrest Young<br>at 120–150 m near Manado, Sulawesi, Indonesia.</p>
<p>Etymology. Named abyssus, a Latinized form of the Greek noun abyssos (meaning “abyss”), to honor the<br>documentary film Pacific Abyss, produced by the British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC), which funded the<br>expedition on which the type specimens were collected. The vernacular name “Deep Blue Chromis”, a refer-<br>ence to both the life color of this species and the relatively (within the context of the genus) deep-dwelling<br>habits, is suggested instead of the more literally translated “Abyss Chromis”, so as not to imply that the spe-<br>cies inhabits depths commonly defined as “abyssal”.</p>
<p>Remarks. This species was first observed by the senior author on May 10, 1997, during a mixed-gas<br>rebreather dive to 120 m on the east side of Augulpelu Reef; Palau (07º 16.41' N, 134º 31.44' E). It was later<br>observed at the same reef at depths of 117–139 m from a submersible by Patrick L. Colin and Lori J. Bell in<br>February–March, 2001. In April 2005, Mr. Forrest Young and colleagues observed several individuals of this<br>(or a very similar) species during mixed-gas rebreather dives at depths of 120–150 m at Manado, Sulawesi,<br>Indonesia. The type specimens included herein are the first of this species to be collected.</p>
<p>From these observations, C. abyssus appears to prefer depths in excess of 115 m, staying close to the sub-<br>stratum among boulders and rock outcroppings, where it takes refuge in small caves and holes. Juveniles and<br>some subadults were also observed around limestone talus. Adults were usually observed singly or in pairs,<br>while subadults and juveniles were seen in small groups. All type specimens were collected in the same gen-<br>eral area, where the species is not uncommon. Other Chromis observed in the vicinity include three of the new<br>species described herein (C. brevirostris, C. degruyi, and C. earina).</p>
<p>Chromis abyssus is not obviously allied with any other known species of the genus. It shares some simi-<br>larities with a group of seven Indo-Pacific deep-dwelling Chromis species, characterized by a similar stout</p>

In their 1985 description of C. abyssicola, Allen and<br>Randall noted a complex of deep-dwelling Chromis species distinguished by, among other characters, 19 or<br>20 pectoral rays, and 28–34 gill rakers. In addition to C. abyssicola, their complex included C. megalopsis78<br>Allen 1976 (now regarded as a junior synonym of C. mirationis Tanaka 1917), C. mirationis Tanaka 1917 and<br>C. struhsakeri Randall and Swerdloff 1973, to which we would add the subsequently named C. planesi79 Lec-<br>chini and Williams 2004. C. abyssus has fewer pectoral rays (18 or 19) and fewer gill rakers (24–28) than<br>members of this species complex, and may comprise a second grouping of deep-dwelling Chromis species,<br>along with C. okamuri Yamakawa and Randall 1989 from Japan, the East African C. woodsi80 Bruner and<br>Arnam 1979 (both easily distinguished from C. abyssus on the basis of color and certain morphological char-<br>acters such as number of gill rakers and tubed lateral-line scales), as well as the two new species C. circumau-<br>rea and C. degruyi, both described herein. Of the remaining two deep-dwelling Indo-Pacific stout-bodied<br>Chromis species with XIV spines, C. onumai Senou and Kudo 2007 has the high pectoral-ray count of the first<br>complex (19–20) and the gill-raker count of the second (25–27). C. axillaris81 (Bennett 1831) has a wide gill-<br>raker range (26–30) and cannot easily be placed in either complex by this character. Of the three new Chromis<br>with XIV dorsal-fin spines described herein (C. abyssus, C. circumaurea, and C. degruyi), each has a unique<br>and distinctive color pattern, and is readily distinguished from the others. Among the three, the former two (C.<br>abyssus and C. circumaurea) share the most similarities both in terms of morphology and in Barcode DNA<br>sequence data.</p>

<p>Diagnosis. Dorsal rays XIII,13–14 (usually 14); anal rays II,15–16; pectoral rays 18–19 (usually 19);<br>spiniform caudal rays 2–3 (usually 3); tubed lateral-line scales 14–16; gill rakers 6–8+19–22 (usually<br>6–7+19–21; total 26–29); body depth 1.57–1.77 in SL; color when fresh pale lavender-tinged gray dorsally,<br>paler ventrally; three or four rows of scales dorsally from nape to upper caudal peduncle with gold edges;<br>small scales on basal sheath of dorsal fin almost entirely gold; median fin membranes lavender gray or trans-<br>lucent blue, suffused with gold color; iris yellow.</p>
<p>Description. <p>Distribution. This species has been observed or collected from the Marshall Islands southward to Fiji,<br>across the Caroline Islands from Puluwat to Palau, and south to Vanuatu. An underwater photo of what seems<br>to be this species (or an undescribed species very similar to C. brevirostris) taken in Bali, Indonesia, appears<br>as “Chromis sp.” on p. 531 of Kuiter &amp; Debelius, 2006. C. brevirostris was not observed at Rarotonga (Cook<br>Islands), Kiritimati (Line Islands), or American Samoa during brief surveys of deep reefs at those localities.</p>
<p>Etymology. Named brevirostris, an adjective derived from the Latin words brevis (meaning “short”) and<br>rostrum (meaning “beak” or “snout”), in reference to the very short snout of this species relative to other spe-<br>cies in the genus.</p>
<p>Remarks. Generally abundant in its typical environment, which is characterized by steep slopes and<br>drop-offs at depths of about 90–120 m. Usually found in association with small holes and limestone talus,<br>often in aggregations ranging from a half-dozen to several dozen individuals feeding low in the water column.</p>
<p>Chromis brevirostris, as well as the new species C. earina (described herein) are both deep-dwelling<br>Chromis species with XIII dorsal spines, a deep body (1.57–1.9 in S.L.), short snout (3.45–4.8 in head), and<br>large eye (2.0–2.6 in head). Most counts are similar, and these two species appear to have more affinities with<br>each other than with any other species in the genus. They can be readily distinguished from each other on the<br>basis of color and by differences in general body shape (particularly the shape of the head profile, which is<br>smoothly convex in C. brevirostris, as opposed to slightly concave dorsal to the eye in C. earina), and dorsal-<br>and anal-fin soft ray counts. Chromis brevirostris has 13–14 dorsal-fin soft rays and 15–16 anal-fin soft rays<br>(except for one deformed individual with only 13 anal-fin soft rays); whereas C. earina has 11–12 dorsal-fin<br>soft rays and 12 anal-fin soft rays. Table 3 lists selected characters for four Indo-Pacific Chromis species<br>which seem to be most similar to C. brevirostris and C. earina. These four species, including C. alpha101 Ran-<br>dall 1988a, C. nigroanalis102 Randall 1988b, C. ovatiformis103 Fowler 1946, and C. pembae104 Randall and<br>McCosker 1992 (which we collectively and informally refer to here as the “Chromis alpha complex”) all have<br>XIII dorsal spines, a roughly similar body shape, and have been recorded at a depth of 40 m or greater. For<br>each of these species, at least one count, and all gill raker counts, range outside the respective ranges for C.<br>brevirostris and C. earina.</p>

<p>Chromis circumaurea, new species</p>
<p>urn:lsid:zoobank.org:act:8ADC4817-8F1C-4C88-8B8A-5372A84CAEC9</p>
<p>Gold-rim Chromis</p>
<p>(Figs. 3a –3c, Ta b l e 4 ; Morphbank105; GenBank106; Barcode107)</p>
<p>Holotype. BPBM 40836108 (98.2 mm SL), Caroline Islands; Yap, S end; “Magic Kingdom” (9&deg;26'3.41&quot;N,<br>138&deg;2'5.96&quot;E): among boulders on sloping shelf above deep drop-off, 98–100 m, hand net, R.L. Pyle and B.D.<br>Greene, 20 April 2007 [PCMB 3080109].</p>
<p>Paratypes. BMNH 2007.10.31.3110 (102.4 mm SL) [PCMB 3081111]. CAS 225757112 (97.6 mm SL)<br>[PCMB 3078113]. MNHN 2007-1924114 (92.5 mm SL) [PCMB 3076115]. USNM 391138116 (94.2 mm SL)<br>[PCMB 3077117]. WAM P.32900-001118 (96.6 mm SL) [PCMB 3079119]. All with same data as holotype.</p>
<p>Diagnosis. Dorsal rays XIV,12–13 (usually 13); anal rays II, 13–14 (usually 13); pectoral rays 18–19;<br>spiniform caudal rays 3; tubed lateral-line scales 16–17; gill rakers 6–7+20–21 (total 26–27); body depth<br>1.68–1.86 in SL; color when fresh mahogany brown with bright yellow distally on spinous portion of dorsal<br>fin; soft portion of dorsal fin, caudal fin, and anal fin bright yellow.</p>
<p>Description. Dorsal rays XIV,13 (12 in one paratype); anal rays II,13 (14 in one paratype); all dorsal and<br>anal rays branched, the last to base in some specimens; pectoral rays 19 (18–19), the upper 2 and lowermost<br>unbranched; pelvic rays I,5; principal caudal rays 8+7=15; upper and lower procurrent caudal rays 5, the ante-<br>rior 3 spiniform, the posterior 2 segmented and unbranched; tubed lateral-line scales 16|17 (16–17); posterior<br>midlateral scales with a pore or deep pit 8 (5–8); scales above dorsal fin to origin of dorsal fin 3.5 (3–3.5);<br>scales below lateral line to origin of anal fin 10 (9–10.5); gill rakers 6+21=27 (6–7+20–21= 26–27); surpaneu-<br>ral (predorsal) bones 3; vertebrae 12+13.</p>
<p>Body moderately deep, depth 1.71 (1.68–1.86) in SL, and compressed, the width 3.02 (2.93–3.22) in body<br>depth; head length 3.28 (3.18–3.37) in SL; dorsal profile of head with slight convexity anterior to eye, slight<br>concavity dorsal to eye, and slight convexity on nape; snout shorter than orbit diameter, its length 3.71<br>(3.82–4.30) in head length; orbit diameter 2.61 (2.35–2.63) in head length; interorbital space convex, its width<br>2.61 (2.54–2.74) in head length; caudal-peduncle depth 2.04 (2.02–2.04) in head; caudal-peduncle length 3.19<br>(2.72–3.40) in head.</p>
<p>Mouth terminal, small, oblique, the upper jaw forming an angle of about 40º to horizontal axis of head and<br>body; posterior edge of maxilla reaching slightly beyond a vertical at anterior edge of pupil, the upper jaw<br>length 3.09 (2.86–3.22) in head; teeth multi-serial, an outer row of conical teeth in each jaw, largest anteriorly;<br>about 32 upper and about 26 lower teeth on each side of jaw; a narrow band of villiform teeth lingual to outer<br>row, in 2–3 irregular rows anteriorly, narrowing to a single row on side of jaws; tongue triangular with<br>rounded tip; gill rakers long and slender, the longest on lower limb near angle about three-fourths length of<br>longest gill filaments; nostril with a fleshy rim, more elevated on posterior edge and located at level of middle<br>of pupil, slightly less than one-third distance from front of snout to base of upper lip.</p>

<p>Opercle ending posteriorly in a flat spine, the tip relatively obtuse and obscured by a large scale; margin<br>of preopercle smooth, the posterior margin extending dorsally to level of upper edge of pupil; suborbital with<br>free lower margin extending nearly to a vertical at posterior edge of pupil.</p>
<p>Scales finely ctenoid; anterior lateral line ending beneath rear portion of spinous dorsal fin (between 12th<br>and 13th dorsal-fin spines); head scaled except lips, tip of snout, and a narrow zone from orbit to edge of snout<br>containing nostrils; a scaly sheath at base of dorsal and anal fins, about two-thirds pupil diameter at base of<br>middle of spinous portion of dorsal fin, progressively narrower on soft portion; a column of scales on each<br>membrane of dorsal fin, narrowing distally, those on spinous portion of dorsal progressively longer, reaching<br>about two-thirds distance to spine tips on posterior membranes; scales on anal-fin membrane in two columns,<br>progressively smaller distally; small scales on caudal fin extending slightly more than two-thirds distance to<br>posterior margin; small scales on basal one-fifth of pectoral fins; a median scaly process extending posteriorly<br>from between base of pelvic fins, its length about half that of pelvic spine; axillary scale above base of pelvic<br>spine slightly more than one-third length of spine.</p>
<p>Origin of dorsal fin over fourth lateral-line scale, the pre-dorsal distance 2.31 (2.30–2.47) in SL; base of<br>spinous portion of dorsal fin contained 2.14 (2.09–2.18) in SL; base of soft portion of dorsal fin contained<br>6.16 (5.68–6.35) in SL; first dorsal spine 12.62 (9.91–10.88) in SL; second dorsal spine 6.83 (6.13–6.88) in<br>SL; third dorsal spine 5.52 (4.81–5.53) in SL; fourth dorsal spine 4.88 (4.73–5.09) in SL; fifth dorsal spine<br>4.86 (4.66–5.02) in SL; sixth dorsal spine 4.82 (4.63–5.08) in SL; last dorsal spine 6.40 (6.13–6.57) in SL;<br>membranes of spinous portion of dorsal fin moderately incised; fourth dorsal soft ray longest, its length 4.20<br>(4.18–4.67) in SL; first anal spine 11.98 (10.50–11.05) in SL; second anal spine 3.77 (3.89–4.15) in SL; first<br>anal soft ray the longest, its length 4.18 (4.31–4.56) in SL; caudal fin forked, its length 3.17 (2.68–3.39) in SL,<br>the caudal concavity 5.46 (5.20–6.44) in SL; fourth pectoral-fin ray longest, 2.77 (2.62–2.83) in SL; pelvic<br>spine 5.78 (5.09–5.52) in SL; first soft ray of pelvic fin without long filamentous extension, usually not reach-<br>ing anal fin, its length 3.89 (3.82–4.20) in SL.</p>
<p>Color when fresh mahogany brown, appearing slate brown underwater; lateral line faintly brownish<br>cream-colored; scales below lateral line with faint brownish cream-colored broad center area, forming<br>approximately eight horizontal stripes visible underwater; spinous portion of dorsal fin same color as body,<br>becoming bright yellow distally on first spine; second through last dorsal spines and membranes abruptly yel-<br>low distally, yellow portion increasing from distal one-fourth of fin at third spine to distal half at eleventh<br>spine; soft dorsal fin entirely bright yellow except for posteriorly diminishing thin brown area basally on ante-<br>rior 6 rays; caudal region from posterior base of dorsal fin to posterior tip of caudal fin uniform bright yellow;<br>brown body color extends posterior to anal fin to lower anterior caudal peduncle; anal fin spines yellowish<br>white; anal fin rays and membranes bright yellow; scales along ventral margin from anus to origin anal fin<br>yellow; pectoral fin translucent; pelvic-fin spine translucent, medial yellow wash on anterior 3 pelvic rays,<br>rays otherwise translucent; pelvic-fin membranes mahogany brown basally, translucent distally; iris brown<br>with yellow wash.</p>
<p>Color in alcohol similar to fresh color, except yellow portions are much paler yellow, and brown portions<br>are slightly paler brown (much paler brown on thorax).</p>
<p>Distribution. Observed from submersibles in the Marshall Islands and Mariana Islands, but only col-<br>lected from Yap.</p>
<p>Etymology. Named circumaurea, an adjective derived from the Latin words circum (meaning “around”)<br>and aurea (meaning “golden, of gold”), in reference to the golden-yellow anal fin, caudal fin, and outer mar-<br>gin of the dorsal fin.</p>

<p>Two photos appearing on p. 390 of Kuiter &amp; Tonozuka (2001), labelled as Chromis analis120 (Cuvier<br>1830), bear a remarkable resemblance to C. circumaurea, but differ in number of dorsal-fin spines (XIII vs.<br>XIV) and color of body (paler in C. analis), caudal peduncle (dark centrally vs. entriely yellow), and central<br>region of caudal fin (transparent vs. yellow). <p>Distribution. Observed or collected throughout the Caroline Islands, from Puluwat to Palau. A Chromis<br>resembling the juvenile of this species was observed in Fagatele Bay in May 2001 by the senior author.</p>
<p>Etymology. Named degruyi to honor Michael V. DeGruy, in recognition of the sincere enthusiasm and<br>determination he demonstrated while attempting to collect the first adult specimen of this species.</p>
<p>Remarks. The habitat of this species is similar to that of other species described herein: deep outer-reef<br>slopes at depths of 85–120 m, usually in the vicinity of rock outcrops with small holes and caves, and around<br>limestone talus. It is generally not as abundant as other species of Chromis described here, usually found in<br>small groups and observed feeding low in the water column.</p>
<p>The species appears most similar to other deep-dwelling species of Chromis described previously (see<br>Remarks section of C. abyssus). Juveniles superficially resemble C. opercularis136 (Günther in Playfair and<br>Günther 1867) in color, but are easily distinguished from that species on several morphological characters<br>(e.g., usually XIV dorsal-fin spines in C. degruyi, vs. XIII in C. opercularis; body depth 1.84–1.99 in SL vs.<br>2.1–2.3 in C. opercularis), as well as adult coloration. Some aspects of the adult coloration are similar to C.<br>planesi Lecchini and Williams 2004, but C. degruyi differs from that species in many other aspects of adult<br>coloration as well as number of pectoral-fin rays (20 in C. planesi vs. 18 in C. degruyi), dorsal-fin soft rays<br>(usually 13 vs. usually 12), and tubed lateral-line scales (17 vs. usually 15–16).</p>

<p>Color of adults and juveniles when fresh pale slate blue (bright pale green in life), slightly darker dorsally<br>and slightly lighter on thorax; a white spot roughly the size of a scale mid-laterally, approximately below the<br>tenth dorsal-fin spine and two scale rows below the lateral line, size and shape of spot variable, occasionally<br>as two small spots in vertical orientation; malachite green area above orbit and in interorbital space, extending<br>diffusely onto nape; bright band of pale turquoise blue below orbit extending across upper lip; spinous portion<br>of dorsal fin color of body, with bright distal border of pale turquoise blue; soft dorsal-fin membrane pale<br>blue, becoming translucent on distal half; caudal fin pale slate blue, becoming lighter and distally translucent<br>on inner rays; caudal fin tips dark, almost black; anal fin pale slate blue, with pale turquoise blue anterior dis-<br>tal border; pectoral fin translucent; yellowish spot about scale size at upper pectoral axil; pelvic fin spine and<br>first-ray filament pale turquoise blue, membranes translucent; iris dark gray with white inner border.</p>
<p>Color in alcohol olive-brown with whitish margins on median fins; white spot on side of body not visible.</p>
<p>Distribution. Collected or observed throughout the tropical western Pacific, from Puluwat westward to<br>Palau, south to Papua New Guinea, Vanuatu and Fiji. It was not observed at Rarotonga (Cook Islands), Kiriti-<br>mati (Line Islands), or American Samoa during brief surveys of deep reefs at those localities. A single speci-<br>men was also recently collected by Mark Erdmann (Conservation International) in 75 m depth at Misool<br>Island, Raja Ampat Islands, Indonesia (G. Allen, pers. comm.).</p>
<p>Etymology. Named earina, a Latinized form of the Greek adjective earinos (meaning “the color of<br>spring”, i.e., green), in reference to the pale green color of this species in life.</p>
<p>Remarks. This species inhabits the same general habitat as the other new species described herein: steep<br>outer reef slopes and drop-offs with rocky outcrops and small caves and holes, often in association with lime-<br>stone talus. It is often observed in pairs or small groups, feeding low in the water column, and is generally<br>abundant where it is found.</p>
<p>Similarities with other species are discussed in the Remarks section under the account of C. brevirostris.</p>
